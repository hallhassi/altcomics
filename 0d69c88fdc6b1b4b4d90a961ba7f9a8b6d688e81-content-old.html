It’s not about the stakes, but about the right to play the game.
It’s a little late, no longer topical, but the feeling that occasionally arises, for example when a discussion about freedom of expression flares up again, remains the same: what bothers me about public cynics and satirists, with the Charlie Hebdo clan at the forefront, is that they are willing to present a lack of humor and imagination as a bitter pill that simply has to be swallowed if one considers oneself a ‘believer in free speech’, assuming moreover that this 'believer in free speech’ needs so-called 'soldiers of free speech’ who, full of adrenaline and with righteousness on their side (as they believe), dutifully delineate the boundaries of this precious principle – because that is what they do! How can I make them understand that I don’t experience these boundaries in the first place, and that I view the 'sacred cows’ that they feel they must tear down as though they had built them specifically for that purpose; an honorable act, they say. I certainly don’t see the humor in this: that when people die on this front (they haven’t pressed the heads firmly enough onto the stakes), I am expected for some reason to feel indebted to the victims, simply because these combative bureaucrats dare to consider themselves the owners of a refined, almost culinary coarse mouth, with which they will supposedly blow some space into the heads of the narrow-minded thinkers, or the potential readers (including me?). I hereby inform anyone who wishes to understand that I am not interested in such interference – your camp is not mine – and that in this matter I am not concerned with honor, but with the stakes involved. Kate Sinha 2018 autotranslated
